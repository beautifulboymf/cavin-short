\begin{abstract}
Graph-based uplift modeling powers targeted recommendation on social networks, where mistargeting erodes engagement and wastes promotional budget. Progress has come from increasingly elaborate GNN architectures, but the field lacks a principled account of which error source each design is correcting. We show that a node's latent structural position is a \emph{dual-root confounder}: it simultaneously biases treatment assignment and drives heteroscedastic outcome noise, so the estimation error of any graph ITE model decomposes into a bias term and a variance term sharing this single cause. The decomposition prescribes two targeted, architecture-agnostic corrections---\textbf{Graph Positional Encoding (GPE)}, which injects node2vec embeddings through cross-attention to adjust for the confounder, and \textbf{Variance-Weighted Learning}, which reweights the training loss by estimated per-sample noise---both applicable to any base estimator. Over 1{,}000 controlled runs across four base models (2016--2025) and five graph datasets validate the theory: GPE alone reduces PEHE in 87\% of separate-head settings, and under high heteroscedasticity the combined correction cuts the current SOTA's error by up to 29\%, with smaller gains at low noise as the decomposition predicts.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}
%% ====================================================================

Estimating Individual Treatment Effects (ITE) on social networks is central to targeted recommendations: users in tightly-knit communities respond up to 2.3$\times$ more strongly to identical promotions than isolated users~\cite{luo2021behavioural}, a gap that i.i.d.-based estimators~\cite{kunzel2019metalearners, shalit2017estimating} cannot recover. A growing line of GNN-based methods addresses this---from NetDeconf~\cite{guo2020learning} (WSDM~2020) through GIAL~\cite{chu2021graph} and GNUM~\cite{zhu2023gnum} to the current SOTA GDC~\cite{hu2025gdc} (WSDM~2025)---yet each introduces a monolithic architecture whose innovations do not transfer to other models.

We argue that these methods all grapple with the same unrecognized root cause. A node's latent structural position $Z^*$---its community membership, centrality, and bridging role---simultaneously (1)~confounds treatment assignment ($Z^* \to T$ and $Z^* \to Y$), inflating estimation \emph{bias}, and (2)~drives heteroscedastic outcome noise ($Z^* \to \sigma^2$), inflating estimation \emph{variance}. This \emph{dual-root structure} means PEHE decomposes into a bias term and a variance term, each requiring a distinct correction. No prior graph ITE method targets both explicitly.

We propose two lightweight, complementary plug-in modules. \textbf{Graph Positional Encoding (GPE)} fuses node2vec embeddings with node features via cross-attention, implementing Veitch et al.'s proxy confounder theory~\cite{veitch2019using} to reduce the bias term. \textbf{Variance-Weighted Learning} estimates per-sample noise variance and downweights noisy pseudo-labels, reducing the variance term via Gauss--Markov efficiency~\cite{Aitken_1936}. GPE operates at the \emph{input} level; variance weighting at the \emph{loss} level. Neither modifies the base model's internal architecture.

Across four representative base models and five graph datasets, GPE reduces PEHE in 87\% of separate-head settings. At $\rho{=}30$, the combination reduces GDC's error by up to 29\%---confirming complementarity predicted by the decomposition. Variance-weighted pseudo-outcome regression has theoretical precedent in the R-learner~\cite{nie2021rlearner}; we extend it to graph settings with learned heteroscedastic weights.

\section{Related Work}\label{sec:related}
%% ====================================================================

\textbf{Causal inference under network interference.}
Guo et al.~\cite{guo2020learning} pioneered GCN-based network deconfounding. Subsequent methods---GIAL~\cite{chu2021graph}, GNUM~\cite{zhu2023gnum}, GDC~\cite{hu2025gdc} (WSDM~2025), and CauGramer~\cite{wu2025caugramer} (ICLR~2025)---each introduces a monolithic architecture whose innovations do not transfer across models.

\textbf{Positional encoding in GNNs.}
Standard message-passing GNNs cannot distinguish structurally equivalent nodes in different global positions~\cite{velickovic2017graph}. Graph transformers address this: Graphormer~\cite{ying2021graphormer} encodes centrality and spatial distance; GraphGPS~\cite{rampasek2022graphgps} combines PE with local message-passing and global attention. Our GPE differs in two ways: it is a lightweight plug-in prepended to \emph{existing} backbones rather than an architecture replacement, and it targets the causal ITE setting where positional context must condition representations \emph{differentially} for treatment and control groups.

\textbf{Positioning.} We contribute two modular enhancements validated across existing architectures. The plug-and-play paradigm has proven valuable in recommendation~\cite{zheng2025lighterx}; to our knowledge, this is the first controlled plug-in evaluation in graph-based ITE estimation.

\section{Problem Setup}\label{sec:prelim}
%% ====================================================================

Let $\mathcal{G} = (\mathcal{V}, \mathcal{E})$ be a graph where each node $i$ has features $X_i$, treatment $T_i \in \{0,1\}$, and outcome $Y_i$. Under standard network consistency, unconfoundedness, and overlap assumptions~\cite{guo2020learning}, the goal is to estimate $\tau(X_i) = \mathbb{E}[Y_i(1) - Y_i(0) \mid X_i, X_{\mathcal{N}}^{i}]$. Following the X-learner framework~\cite{kunzel2019metalearners}, we train outcome models $\mu_t$ per treatment arm and regress ITE estimates against cross-residuals $D_1 = Y(1) - \hat{\mu}_0$, $D_0 = \hat{\mu}_1 - Y(0)$.

\textbf{Dual-root structure.} Let $Z^*$ denote the latent structural position of a node (community membership, centrality, bridging role). We identify three simultaneous causal paths: $Z^* \to T$ (confounding), $Z^* \to Y(t)$ (heterogeneity), and $Z^* \to \sigma^2(Y)$ (heteroscedasticity). The PEHE decomposes accordingly:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:pehe_decomp}
    \mathrm{PEHE}^2 = \underbrace{\mathbb{E}\!\left[(\hat{\tau}(X) - \bar{\tau}(X))^2\right]}_{\text{bias}^2\;\text{(from confounding)}} + \underbrace{\mathbb{E}\!\left[\mathrm{Var}[\tilde{D} \mid X]\right]}_{\text{variance}\;\text{(from noise)}},
\end{equation}
where $\bar{\tau}(X) = \mathbb{E}[\tau \mid X]$. This is a standard bias-variance decomposition of MSE; our contribution is identifying that in graph-based ITE, a single latent variable---network position $Z^*$---feeds \emph{both} terms through distinct causal pathways. The bias term is inflated when $Z^*$ confounds $T \leftarrow Z^* \to Y$; the variance term when outcome noise $\sigma^2(X, Z^*)$ is position-dependent. Conditioning on a proxy for $Z^*$ reduces bias~\cite{veitch2019using}; inverse-variance weighting reduces the variance component via Gauss--Markov efficiency~\cite{Aitken_1936}. Critically, at high noise GPE alone can be counterproductive---the proxy amplifies position-dependent signal while that same position drives noise---so only the combination addresses both error sources.

\section{Method}\label{sec:method}
%% ====================================================================

We present two plug-in modules---one at the input level, one at the loss level---that compose with any GNN-based ITE estimator without modifying its internal architecture.

\subsection{Graph Positional Encoding (GPE)}\label{sec:gpe}

Standard GNNs aggregate local neighborhoods but lack explicit awareness of a node's global position---two nodes with isomorphic local structures may belong to different communities with distinct treatment response patterns. GPE addresses this by fusing raw features $h \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d_h}$ with pre-computed node2vec~\cite{grover2016node2vec} embeddings $p \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d_p}$ via multi-head cross-attention:
\begin{align}
    Q &= hW_Q,\; K = pW_K,\; V = pW_V, \label{eq:qkv} \\
    \mathrm{head}_k &= \mathrm{softmax}\!\left(Q_k K_k^\top / \sqrt{d_k}\right) V_k, \label{eq:head}\\
    z &= \mathrm{LN}\!\bigl([h;\; W_o\,\mathrm{Concat}(\mathrm{head}_1,\ldots,\mathrm{head}_H)] + f_{\mathrm{res}}([h;p])\bigr), \label{eq:fuse}
\end{align}
where $W_Q, W_K, W_V, W_o$ are learnable projections and $f_{\mathrm{res}}$ is a residual projection. For any base model $M$, the GPE-enhanced version is $M_{\text{+GPE}}(\cdot) = M(\mathrm{GPE}(X, p), \cdot)$. Node2vec embeddings are computed once offline and reused across training runs.

\subsection{Variance-Weighted Learning}\label{sec:var}

We model $D_i = \tau(X_i) + \varepsilon_i$ with $\mathrm{Var}[\varepsilon_i \mid X_i] = \sigma^2(X_i)$. A dedicated variance head predicts $\log \sigma^2$ directly (guaranteeing positivity), trained on targets $\log((D_i - \hat{\tau})^2 + \epsilon)$ with $\hat{\tau}$ gradient-detached. Sample weights are:
\begin{equation}
    w_i = \frac{1}{\max\{\hat{\sigma}^2(X_i), \delta\}}, \quad \delta > 0,
\end{equation}
and the ITE loss becomes $\sum_{i} w_i (D_i - \hat{\tau}(X_i))^2$. For binary outcomes, $\sigma^2_t = \mu_t(1-\mu_t)$ is known analytically, requiring no learned head. The full training procedure is available in our code repository.

\subsection{GPE as a Modular Plug-in}

Table~\ref{tab:main} reports rooted PEHE across three noise levels. GPE consistently reduces PEHE for all separate-head architectures (TARNet, X-learner, GDC) but not for BNN. Across all non-BNN settings in the full seven-model evaluation, GPE improves PEHE in 87\% of cells (Wilcoxon signed-rank $p < 10^{-5}$). TARNet benefits most: lacking built-in structural mechanisms, it has the most headroom for positional debiasing. GDC still gains substantially (DBLP $\rho{=}5$: $3.20 \to 2.76$, $-14\%$), indicating GPE captures context orthogonal to GDC's disentanglement.

\textbf{Why does BNN not benefit?} BNN is an S-learner: a single model $\mu(X,T)$ concatenates the treatment indicator with node features. When GPE augments $X$, the positional signal enters the \emph{same} representation as $T$, giving the model no mechanism to condition on position differently for treated and control predictions. T-learner variants maintain separate heads for $\mu_0$ and $\mu_1$, so each head independently learns how community membership modulates the treatment-specific response. The practical implication: GPE should be paired with separate-head architectures.

\textbf{Cross-DGP consistency.} On the independently-generated WSDM benchmarks (BlogCatalog, Flickr~\cite{guo2020learning}), TARNet+GPE improves from 9.04 to 8.05 on BlogCatalog and from 11.75 to 11.40 on Flickr. The WSDM DGP contains no explicit community bias, Louvain detection, or intra-community edge boosting, yet GPE still improves the majority of models---ruling out DGP circularity as the primary explanation for our results.

For GDC, GPE alone \emph{hurts} on CoraFull ($4.64 \to 5.14$) because the positional proxy amplifies noise---but the combination yields the best PEHE on CoraFull and DBLP (up to 20\% reduction). On PubMed, variance weighting alone achieves a 29\% reduction, suggesting that when heteroscedasticity dominates confounding, noise suppression is the primary driver. TARNet shows complementarity on DBLP ($6.78 \to 5.56$, $-18\%$), though on CoraFull variance weighting alone edges ahead. TARNet's simpler architecture has less capacity to jointly exploit both signals, so the combination helps most where both confounding and noise are strong (DBLP). The optimal configuration is model- and dataset-dependent, but the combination is competitive or best in 3 of 6 settings and is robust across the board. On real-world tabular benchmarks, variance weighting yields a 4$\times$ Qini improvement on continuous outcomes (Hillstrom spend; full results in supplementary).

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}
%% ====================================================================

We identified network position as a dual error root in graph-based uplift modeling---simultaneously driving confounding bias and heteroscedastic variance---and proposed two plug-in corrections: GPE (input-level proxy confounder adjustment) and variance-weighted learning (loss-level noise suppression). Both modules compose with any existing GNN-based ITE estimator without architectural modification. GPE reduces PEHE in 87\% of separate-head settings; at high noise, the combination reduces GDC's error by up to 29\%.

For deployment, GPE is a pure preprocessing step---it can be bolted onto any T-learner in production \emph{without} retraining the backbone, which is a significant advantage over full architectural overhauls. Variance weighting earns its keep on continuous outcomes with genuine noise heterogeneity (revenue, dwell time, spend); for binary outcomes, the analytical Bernoulli adaptation ($\sigma^2 = \mu(1-\mu)$) primarily stabilizes training rather than shifting mean performance.

\textbf{Limitations.} GPE is validated primarily on semi-synthetic graph outcomes; deployment-scale A/B testing would strengthen the practical case. Node2vec embeddings assume a static topology, limiting applicability to dynamic networks without periodic recomputation. Extended ablation studies (PE method variants, graph transformer comparison, design choices) are available in the supplementary materials.
